\begin{abstract}
\noindent
\textsf{emphasis} is an \textsf{R} package for studying species diversification
and its ecological drivers. It implements a general birth--death model in which
per-lineage speciation and extinction rates depend on arbitrary state covariates
--- current species richness, total phylogenetic diversity, and lineage-specific
evolutionary time. Because the marginal likelihood over the unobserved extinct
lineages is analytically intractable, the package uses \emph{importance sampling
on augmented trees}: a proposal distribution augments the observed extant tree
with stochastically drawn extinct lineages, and the resulting Monte Carlo
estimate of the marginal log-likelihood serves as the objective for two
complementary optimisers --- a Monte Carlo Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) for
global search and a Monte Carlo EM (MCEM) algorithm for local refinement.
Model selection uses the Akaike Information Criterion computed from the IS
log-likelihood estimate at the optimum.
This document gives a self-contained mathematical derivation of every component.
\end{abstract}

\section{The Diversification Model}\label{sec:model}

\subsection{General rate specification}

The \textsf{emphasis} model belongs to the family of state-dependent
birth--death processes \citep{etienne2012prolonged,janzen2015diversity}.
Every lineage $s$ alive at time $t$ (measured forward from the crown) is
subject to a per-lineage speciation rate and extinction rate that depend on
the current macroecological state of the clade:

\begin{align}\label{eq:rates}
  \lambda(s,t;\pars) &= f\!\left(\beta_0 + \beta_N N(t)
                         + \beta_P P(t) + \beta_E E(s,t)\right), \nonumber\\
  \mu(s,t;\pars)     &= f\!\left(\gamma_0 + \gamma_N N(t)
                         + \gamma_P P(t) + \gamma_E E(s,t)\right),
\end{align}

where the full parameter vector is
$\pars = (\beta_0, \beta_N, \beta_P, \beta_E,
          \gamma_0, \gamma_N, \gamma_P, \gamma_E)^\top \in \R^8$.

\paragraph{State covariates.}
The three covariates in \eqref{eq:rates} capture distinct ecological signals:

\begin{description}
  \item[$N(t)$] \emph{Species richness.} The number of lineages currently alive.
    Negative $\beta_N$ produces diversity-dependent suppression of speciation
    (ecological saturation), while positive $\beta_N$ encodes positive
    diversity feedbacks.
  \item[$P(t)$] \emph{Phylogenetic diversity (PD).} The sum of pendant
    branch-lengths at time $t$:
    \begin{equation}
      P(t) = \sum_{s:\text{alive at }t} E(s,t).
    \end{equation}
    PD measures the ecological breadth available to the clade; high PD
    with low $N$ may permit faster diversification.
  \item[$E(s,t)$] \emph{Evolutionary pendant time.} The time elapsed since
    the lineage $s$ last diverged. Lineages with large $E$ have had longer
    to accumulate reproductive isolation, making them more likely to speciate
    (positive $\beta_E$) or, if the environment has changed, to go extinct
    (positive $\gamma_E$).
\end{description}

\paragraph{Link function.}
The link function $f:\R\to[0,\infty)$ ensures non-negative rates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:link}
  f(\eta) =
  \begin{dcases}
    \max(0, \eta) & \text{linear link (default)}, \\
    \exp(\eta)    & \text{exponential link}.
  \end{dcases}
\end{equation}
The linear link is piecewise-linear and allows rates to reach exactly zero;
the exponential link guarantees strictly positive rates at the cost of less
interpretable coefficients.

\subsection{Special cases (model shortcuts)}\label{sec:models}

By setting subsets of $\{\beta_N, \beta_P, \beta_E, \gamma_N, \gamma_P,
\gamma_E\}$ to zero, equation \eqref{eq:rates} reduces to well-known models
(Table~\ref{tab:models}).

\section{The Phylogenetic Likelihood}\label{sec:likelihood}

\subsection{Observed versus augmented data}

The observed data $\obs$ consists of the extant tree: branching times
$\brts$ and the number of surviving lineages $n$. Because the birth--death
process generates extinct lineages that are not observed, the
\emph{marginal} likelihood
\begin{equation}\label{eq:marginal}
  p(\obs \mid \pars) = \int p(\obs, z \mid \pars) \, \dd\nu(z)
\end{equation}
integrates over the space of all possible extinct-lineage histories $z$,
with $\nu$ a dominating measure on augmented-tree space. This integral is
analytically tractable only for the constant-rate (CR) and some
diversity-dependent (DD) models; for EP and general covariate models it is
intractable.

\subsection{Complete-data log-likelihood}\label{sec:loglik}

Given an augmented tree $\aug = (\obs, z)$ with $K$ total lineages (extant
and extinct), lineage $k$ alive on the interval $[a_k, d_k]$ where $a_k$ is
its birth time and $d_k$ its death time (or the crown age for extant lineages),
the complete-data log-likelihood of the birth--death process is

\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
  \log p(\obs, z \mid \pars)
  = \underbrace{\sum_{k=1}^{K}\sum_{\text{events of }k}
      \log r_k(t_{\text{event}})}_{\text{event contribution}}
    - \underbrace{\sum_{k=1}^{K}
      \int_{a_k}^{d_k}
      \bigl[\lambda(k,t;\pars) + \mu(k,t;\pars)\bigr]\dd t}_{\text{survival contribution}},
\end{equation}

where $r_k(t)$ is the rate of whatever event (speciation or extinction)
terminates lineage $k$ at time $t$. Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Each speciation event of lineage $k$ at time $t$ contributes
    $\log \lambda(k, t; \pars)$.
  \item Each extinction event of lineage $k$ at time $t$ contributes
    $\log \mu(k, t; \pars)$.
  \item The integrated hazard for lineage $k$ over its lifetime is
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard}
      H_k(\pars) = \int_{a_k}^{d_k}
        \bigl[\lambda(k,t;\pars) + \mu(k,t;\pars)\bigr]\dd t.
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Closed-form integrals.}
For the linear link, the integral $H_k(\pars)$ has a closed form for all
supported covariate combinations. For the EP covariate with exponential link,
the integral $\int \exp(\gamma_0 + \gamma_E E(k,t))\dd t$ requires
$E(k,t) = t - a_k$, giving $\int_{a_k}^{d_k} e^{\gamma_0 + \gamma_E(t-a_k)}\dd t$
which is analytically tractable, but combining EP with other exponential-link
covariates is not currently supported.

\subsection{The proposal distribution}\label{sec:proposal}

The proposal $q(z \mid \obs, \pars)$ draws extinct lineages conditioned on
the observed extant tree $\obs$ using a \emph{thinning} (rejection sampling)
approach on a Poisson process with rate bounded by $\lambda_{\max}$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item For each inter-event interval of the extant tree, extinct-lineage
    candidates are generated as events of a homogeneous Poisson process with
    rate $\lambda_{\max}$.
  \item Each candidate time $t^*$ is accepted as an actual extinction event
    with probability $\lambda(t^*;\pars) / \lambda_{\max}$.
  \item Accepted extinctions initiate new lineages that are recursively
    simulated until they go extinct before the present.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting log-proposal probability is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:logq}
  \log q(z \mid \obs, \pars) = \log q_{\rm aug}(z \mid \obs)
  + \text{correction for thinning acceptance probabilities},
\end{equation}
and is computed analytically during the simulation so that it is available
alongside $\logf$ for importance-sampling reweighting.

\section{Importance-Sampling Likelihood Estimation}\label{sec:IS}

\subsection{The IS estimator}

Let $z_1, \ldots, z_S \stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim} q(z \mid \obs, \pars)$.
The marginal likelihood \eqref{eq:marginal} can be written as the expectation
\begin{equation}
  p(\obs \mid \pars)
  = \E_{q}\!\left[\frac{p(\obs, z \mid \pars)}{q(z \mid \obs, \pars)}\right],
\end{equation}
which is estimated without bias by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:IS}
  \Lhat_S(\pars) = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S}
    \exp\!\bigl(\logf_i - \logq_i\bigr)
    = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(\lw_i),
\end{equation}
where $\lw_i = \logf_i - \logq_i$ are the IS log-weights. The IS
log-likelihood estimate is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fhat}
  \fhat(\pars) = \log \Lhat_S(\pars)
  = \log\!\left(\frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(\lw_i)\right).
\end{equation}
Equation \eqref{eq:fhat} is evaluated in numerically stable form using the
log-sum-exp identity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:logsumexp}
  \fhat(\pars) = w_{\max} + \log\!\left(\frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S}
    \exp(\lw_i - w_{\max})\right), \quad
  w_{\max} = \max_i \lw_i.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[Jensen's inequality bias]
Because $\log$ is concave, Jensen's inequality gives
$\fhat(\pars) = \log \E_q[\exp(\lw)] \leq \E_q[\lw]$.
The estimator $\fhat$ is therefore a downward-biased estimate of
$\log p(\obs \mid \pars)$, though the bias vanishes as $S\to\infty$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bias-corrected estimator}\label{sec:biascorrect}

The exact identity relating $\log\E[X]$ to the cumulants of $\log X$ yields
a moment-based correction. Let $\ell_{\rm hat} = \frac{1}{S}\sum_i \lw_i$
and define the central moments
\begin{equation}
  m_k = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S}\bigl(\lw_i - \ell_{\rm hat}\bigr)^k.
\end{equation}
Then the bias-corrected estimator truncated at order $K$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:biascorrect}
  \fhat_{\rm BC}(\pars) = \ell_{\rm hat}
    + \log\!\left(1 + \sum_{k=2}^{K}\frac{m_k}{k!}\right).
\end{equation}
At $K=2$ this reduces to the log-normal correction
$\ell_{\rm hat} + \log(1 + m_2/2) \approx \ell_{\rm hat} + m_2/2$
for small $m_2$. As $K\to\infty$, $\fhat_{\rm BC} \to \fhat$ identically;
the finite-$K$ truncation is the approximation.

\paragraph{Handling rejected trees.}
A tree is rejected if augmentation exceeds the limit $\lambda_{\max}$ or
the maximum extinct-lineage count. Rejected trees have likelihood $L_i = 0$
(i.e.\ $\lw_i = -\infty$) and are excluded from the moment sums but enter
the denominator:
\begin{equation}
  \fhat(\pars) = \fhat_{\rm valid}(\pars) + \log\!\left(\frac{S_{\rm valid}}{S}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\fhat_{\rm valid}$ is computed over the $S_{\rm valid}$ non-rejected
trees only.

\subsection{Effective Sample Size}

The IS effective sample size quantifies how many of the $S$ samples
contribute meaningfully to the estimate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ESS}
  \ESS = \frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(\lw_i)\right)^2}
              {\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(2\lw_i)}.
\end{equation}
$\ESS \approx S$ indicates roughly uniform weights (high IS efficiency);
$\ESS \approx 1$ indicates one tree dominates (IS collapse). We report
$\ESS/S \in (0,1]$ as a diagnostic fraction. For reliable estimation,
$\ESS/S \geq 0.1$ is recommended.

\subsection{Cross-importance sampling (shared-tree mode)}\label{sec:crossIS}

In shared-tree mode (Mode~2), a single pool of $S$ augmented trees
$\{z_1,\ldots,z_S\}$ is drawn at a reference parameter $\pars_0$, and
$\fhat$ is evaluated at any $\pars_j$ via

\begin{equation}\label{eq:crossIS}
  \fhat(\pars_j) = \log\!\left(\frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^{S}
    \exp\bigl(\logf(z_i,\pars_j) - \logq_i\bigr)\right),
\end{equation}

where $\logq_i = \log q(z_i \mid \obs, \pars_0)$ are fixed at simulation
time and $\logf(z_i,\pars_j)$ is re-evaluated at the new $\pars_j$ via
\texttt{eval\_logf()}. This avoids re-simulating trees for each particle in
the population, at the cost of increased variance when $\pars_j$ and
$\pars_0$ are far apart.

\section{The Cross-Entropy Method for Parameter Estimation}\label{sec:CEM}

\subsection{Overview}

The Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) \citep{rubinstein1999cross,deBoer2005tutorial}
solves the optimisation problem
\begin{equation}\label{eq:opt}
  \hat{\pars} = \argmax_{\pars \in [\lb,\,\ub]} \fhat(\pars)
\end{equation}
by maintaining a population of $M$ candidate parameter vectors
(``particles'') and iteratively concentrating them near the optimum. The
key idea is to minimise the KL divergence between the empirical distribution
of high-quality particles and the parametric family used to generate them.

\subsection{Algorithm}\label{sec:CEMalg}

Let $[\lb, \ub] \subset \R^8$ be the search box, $M$ the population size,
$\rho \in (0,1)$ the elite fraction, and $\bm{\sigma}^{(0)} \in \R^8_{>0}$
the initial perturbation standard deviations.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Monte Carlo CEM (\texttt{emphasis\_cem})}\label{alg:CEM}
\DontPrintSemicolon
\KwIn{branching times $\brts$; bounds $[\lb,\ub]$; population size $M$;
      elite fraction $\rho$; IS sample size $S$; initial SD $\bm{\sigma}^{(0)}$;
      annealing step $\alpha$; max iterations $T$; plateau tolerance $\delta$;
      patience $P$}
\KwOut{best parameter estimate $\hat{\pars}$; convergence history}
\BlankLine
Initialise $\{\pars_j^{(0)}\}_{j=1}^{M}$ uniformly in $[\lb,\ub]$\;
$k \leftarrow 0$, \textit{plateau\_count} $\leftarrow 0$\;
\While{$k < T$}{
  \tcc{E-step: evaluate IS log-likelihood for every particle}
  \For{$j = 1, \ldots, M$}{
    Draw $z_1^{(j)},\ldots,z_S^{(j)} \sim q(\cdot \mid \obs, \pars_j^{(k)})$\;
    Compute $\fhat(\pars_j^{(k)})$ via \eqref{eq:fhat}\;
  }
  Mark particle $j$ as invalid if $\fhat(\pars_j^{(k)}) = \mathrm{NA}$\;
  \BlankLine
  \tcc{Record best particle}
  $\hat{\pars}^{(k)} \leftarrow \argmax_j \fhat(\pars_j^{(k)})$\;
  $f^{(k)} \leftarrow \fhat(\hat{\pars}^{(k)})$\;
  \BlankLine
  \tcc{Stopping rules}
  \lIf{$\bm{\sigma}^{(k)} \leq \mathbf{0}$}{\textbf{stop} (\textit{annealing})}
  \If{$k > 0$ and $f^{(k)} - f^{(k-1)} < \delta$}{
    \textit{plateau\_count} $\leftarrow$ \textit{plateau\_count} $+ 1$\;
    \lIf{\textit{plateau\_count} $\geq P$}{\textbf{stop} (\textit{plateau})}
  }\lElse{\textit{plateau\_count} $\leftarrow 0$}
  \BlankLine
  \tcc{M-step: resample around elite particles}
  Sort particles by $\fhat$; retain top $\lfloor\rho M\rfloor$ elite particles\;
  Resample $M - \lfloor\rho M\rfloor$ new particles by perturbing elite copies:
  \[
    \pars_j^{(k+1)} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left(
      \pars_{\pi(j)}^{(k)} + \bm{\epsilon}_j,\; \lb,\; \ub\right),
    \quad \bm{\epsilon}_j \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\,
    \mathrm{diag}(\bm{\sigma}^{(k)})^2)
  \]
  where $\pi(j)$ indexes a uniformly drawn elite particle\;
  Anneal: $\bm{\sigma}^{(k+1)} \leftarrow \bm{\sigma}^{(k)} - \alpha$\;
  $k \leftarrow k + 1$\;
}
$\hat{\pars} \leftarrow \argmax_k \hat{\pars}^{(k)}$ \tcp*{global best across all iterations}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Stopping rules}

Three stopping criteria are checked in order at the end of each iteration:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Annealing exhausted.} $\bm{\sigma}^{(k)} \leq \mathbf{0}$
    element-wise. The population has fully collapsed onto its current best
    point; further perturbation would be zero.
  \item \textbf{Plateau.} The improvement in the best IS log-likelihood,
    $f^{(k)} - f^{(k-1)}$, has been less than $\delta$ (default $10^{-4}$)
    for $P$ (default 5) consecutive iterations. This guards against wasting
    computation when the surface is flat near the optimum.
  \item \textbf{Hard cap.} $k = T$ (default $T = 20$). Unconditional
    maximum-iteration limit.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Particle rescue under limit exceedance}

If all $M$ particles return invalid $\fhat$ (every augmented tree rejected),
the limits are escalated geometrically:
$\lambda_{\max} \leftarrow 10 \lambda_{\max}$,
$N_{\max} \leftarrow 10 N_{\max}$,
and evaluation is retried. If $N_{\max}$ exceeds $10{,}000$ with all
particles still invalid, the run is terminated with
\texttt{stop\_reason = "all\_failed"} and a warning advising the user to
increase \texttt{num\_points} or widen the bounds.

\subsection{Annealing schedule}

The initial perturbation SD is set to
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_p^{(0)} = \frac{u_p - \ell_p}{4}, \quad p = 1, \ldots, 8,
\end{equation}
where $u_p$ and $\ell_p$ are the upper and lower bounds for parameter $p$.
At each iteration the SD is reduced by $\alpha$:
\begin{equation}
  \bm{\sigma}^{(k+1)} = \bm{\sigma}^{(k)} - \alpha \mathbf{1},
  \quad \alpha = \frac{\sigma_p^{(0)}}{T}.
\end{equation}
This linear schedule ensures the population collapses smoothly from
broad exploration to local exploitation over $T$ iterations.

\section{Monte Carlo EM for Local Refinement}\label{sec:MCEM}

\subsection{The EM objective}

The Monte Carlo Expectation--Maximisation (MCEM) algorithm
\citep{wei1990monte,levine2001implementations} iteratively maximises the
expected complete-data log-likelihood
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Qfunc}
  Q(\pars \mid \pars^{(t)}) = \E_{z \mid \obs, \pars^{(t)}}\!
    \left[\log p(\obs, z \mid \pars)\right].
\end{equation}

\subsection{E-step}

In the E-step, $S$ augmented trees are drawn from
$q(z \mid \obs, \pars^{(t)})$ and used to form the IS-weighted approximation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Qhat}
  \hat{Q}(\pars \mid \pars^{(t)})
  = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(\lw_i^{(t)}) \cdot
          \logf(z_i, \pars)}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{S} \exp(\lw_i^{(t)})},
\end{equation}
where $\lw_i^{(t)} = \logf(z_i, \pars^{(t)}) - \logq_i^{(t)}$ are the IS
log-weights at the current iterate $\pars^{(t)}$, and the denominator
normalises them to proper weights.

\subsection{M-step}

The M-step maximises $\hat{Q}(\pars \mid \pars^{(t)})$ over $\pars \in
[\lb, \ub]$ using the COBYLA (Constrained Optimisation BY Linear
Approximations) algorithm \citep{powell1994direct} via the
\texttt{nloptr} interface. Because COBYLA does not require gradient
information, it is robust to the Monte Carlo noise in $\hat{Q}$.

\subsection{Convergence}

MCEM convergence is assessed by monitoring the change in the IS
log-likelihood $\fhat(\pars^{(t)})$ across iterations. The algorithm
terminates when this change falls below a tolerance $\varepsilon$ (default
$10^{-1}$) for a specified number of consecutive post-burnin iterations.
A burn-in phase (default 20 iterations) is excluded from convergence
monitoring to allow initial transients to decay.

\subsection{MCEM as a refinement stage}

Because MCEM converges to a local optimum of $\pars \mapsto
\log p(\obs \mid \pars)$, it is recommended as a \emph{second stage}
after the CEM has located a good region of the parameter space:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Run CEM to obtain $\hat{\pars}_{\rm CEM}$.
  \item Initialise MCEM at $\hat{\pars}_{\rm CEM}$ and refine.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Model Selection}\label{sec:modelsel}

\subsection{AIC}

For each fitted model $\mathcal{M}_k$ with $p_k$ free parameters and IS
log-likelihood $\fhat(\hat{\pars}_k)$, the Akaike Information Criterion is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:AIC}
  \AIC_k = -2\,\fhat(\hat{\pars}_k) + 2 p_k.
\end{equation}
The preferred model minimises $\AIC$. The $\Delta\AIC$ values
$\AIC_k - \min_j \AIC_j \geq 0$ quantify the evidence against model $k$.

\begin{remark}[IS bias and AIC]
Because $\fhat$ is a downward-biased estimate of $\log p(\obs \mid \hat{\pars})$
(Remark~1), the AIC is upward-biased relative to its true value. When
comparing models evaluated at the \emph{same} sample of augmented trees this
bias is constant across models and cancels in $\Delta\AIC$; when trees are
simulated independently per model (the default), residual Monte Carlo noise
may affect comparisons with very small $\Delta\AIC$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bootstrap variance of $\fhat$}

To quantify IS Monte Carlo uncertainty at the optimum, the package provides
a leave-half-out (subsampling) bootstrap variance of $\fhat$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bootvar}
  \widehat{\Var}[\fhat(\hat{\pars})] = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B}
    \bigl(\fhat^{(b)}(\hat{\pars}) - \bar{\fhat}(\hat{\pars})\bigr)^2,
\end{equation}
where $\fhat^{(b)}$ uses a randomly drawn half of the $S_{\rm final}$
augmented trees. A large $\widehat{\Var}$ relative to $|\Delta\AIC|/2$
indicates that the model comparison is noise-dominated and more trees should
be used.

\subsection{Three-model automated selection}

The function \texttt{select\_diversification\_model()} fits the three
biologically most common models (DD, PD, EP) in a single call, applies
\eqref{eq:AIC}, and returns the ranked comparison table together with the
best-fit parameter estimates.

\section{Stochastic Simulation of Phylogenies}\label{sec:sim}

\subsection{Forward simulation}

Forward simulation of the state-dependent birth--death process proceeds by
Gillespie's direct method \citep{gillespie1977exact}. At each step:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute the total event rate
    $R(t) = \sum_s [\lambda(s,t;\pars) + \mu(s,t;\pars)]$.
  \item Draw the waiting time $\Delta t \sim \mathrm{Exp}(R(t))$.
  \item Choose the event type proportional to individual rates.
  \item Update the state $(N, P, \{E_s\})$ and the lineage set.
\end{enumerate}

Because the rates $\lambda$ and $\mu$ depend on $N(t)$, $P(t)$, and
individual $E(s,t)$ which change continuously, the Gillespie step requires
re-computing $R(t)$ at each event. For the linear link with N- and
P-dependence the waiting-time distribution still has a closed form; for EP
dependence the waiting time is drawn via thinning.

\subsection{Conditional simulation (augmentation)}

Given an observed extant tree $\obs$, conditional simulation draws the
extinct lineages $z$ from $q(z \mid \obs, \pars)$ (Section~\ref{sec:proposal}).
This is the E-step of MCEM and the particle evaluation step of CEM.
The C++ engine returns both the augmented tree and $\logq$ so that IS
reweighting can be applied immediately.

\subsection{Batch simulation and survival probability}

Passing a matrix of parameter rows to \texttt{simulate\_tree()} triggers
batch mode: each row is simulated independently, and the fraction of
simulations that produce a non-extinct clade estimates the survival
probability $\Pr(\text{clade survives to present} \mid \pars)$. This
probability is also used to correct the marginal likelihood for conditioning
on non-extinction.

\section{Diagnostics}\label{sec:diagnostics}

\subsection{CEM convergence diagnostics}

The function \texttt{diagnose\_cem()} extracts and plots four diagnostic
panels from a fitted CEM object:

\begin{description}
  \item[Log-likelihood trace.] $f^{(k)}$ versus iteration $k$, annotated
    with the stopping rule. A monotone increasing curve ending at a plateau
    indicates good behaviour.
  \item[Parameter traces.] $\hat{\pars}^{(k)}_p$ versus $k$ for each
    parameter dimension $p$. Stable late-iteration values indicate that the
    population has converged.
  \item[Population spread.] The median and interquartile range of
    $\fhat(\pars_j^{(k)})$ over all valid particles. Narrowing IQR
    reflects progressive population collapse.
  \item[IS weight distribution.] Histogram of $\lw_i$ at the globally best
    particle, annotated with $\ESS$. A concentrated histogram near the mean
    indicates healthy IS weights.
\end{description}

\subsection{GAM-based survival probability}

For DD models, \texttt{train\_GAM()} fits a binomial generalised additive
model (GAM) to the outcomes of batch forward simulations, estimating
$\Pr(\text{survival} \mid \pars)$ as a smooth function of the parameter
space. \texttt{predict\_survival()} then evaluates this surface at any
$\pars$ without re-simulating, enabling fast computation of likelihood
corrections for conditioning on non-extinction.

\section{Implementation}\label{sec:impl}

\subsection{Software architecture}

\textsf{emphasis} is written in \textsf{R} and \textsf{C++}, with the
computationally intensive steps delegated to \textsf{C++} via \textsf{Rcpp}
and parallelised using \textsf{RcppParallel}. The public API consists of
five primary functions:

\begin{description}
  \item[\texttt{simulate\_tree()}] Forward and conditional simulation of
    birth--death trees; dispatches to the C++ engine.
  \item[\texttt{augment\_trees()}] Raw E-step: draw $S$ augmented trees
    from $q(z \mid \obs, \pars)$, returning \texttt{trees}, \texttt{logf},
    and \texttt{logg}.
  \item[\texttt{eval\_logf()}] Re-score pre-computed augmented trees at any
    $\pars$, enabling cross-IS (Section~\ref{sec:crossIS}).
  \item[\texttt{estimate\_rates()}] Unified inference entry point: resolves
    the model, dispatches to CEM or MCEM, and returns a named
    \texttt{emphasis\_fit} object.
  \item[\texttt{compare\_models()} / \texttt{select\_diversification\_model()}]\mbox{}\\
    AIC-based model comparison.
\end{description}

\subsection{Separation of concerns}

The key architectural principle is the separation of simulation and scoring:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{augment\_trees()} simulates trees and computes both $\logf$
    and $\logq$ during the simulation pass.
  \item \texttt{eval\_logf()} re-evaluates $\logf(z, \pars')$ for any new
    $\pars'$ on already-simulated trees, without re-running the stochastic
    augmentation.
  \item IS aggregation is performed in \textsf{R} via
    \texttt{.is\_fhat(logf, logg)}.
\end{enumerate}

This design enables shared-tree mode (Mode~2): a single simulation pass at
$\pars_0$ produces trees that are reused for all particles in the CEM
population, reducing the total number of augmentations from $M \times S$ to
$S$ per iteration, at the cost of higher IS variance.

\subsection{Parameter layout}

Internally, all functions operate on the full 8-element parameter vector
$\pars \in \R^8$ in the order
$(\beta_0, \beta_N, \beta_P, \beta_E, \gamma_0, \gamma_N, \gamma_P, \gamma_E)$.
Model shortcuts set the inactive elements to zero. The model binary flag
$\mathbf{m} = (m_N, m_P, m_E) \in \{0,1\}^3$ indicates which covariates
are active and gates the corresponding rate functions in the C++ likelihood
kernel. For the N and P covariates the model flag only affects the proposal
(which covariate terms to simulate); for the EP covariate, $m_E = 1$ also
switches the rate functions from standard to EP-aware in
\texttt{eval\_logf()}.

\section{Simulation Studies}\label{sec:simstudies}

\subsection{Estimation power}\label{sec:study1}

To evaluate the frequentist accuracy of CEM estimation under the PD model,
we simulate $n = 100$ trees from the true parameter vector
\[
  \pars^* = (\beta_0, \beta_P, \gamma_0, \gamma_P)
          = (0.6,\; 0.005,\; 0.15,\; -0.003)
\]
with crown age $T = 8$, and estimate $\pars$ on each replicate.

For each replicate $r = 1, \ldots, n$ we record the estimate $\hat{\pars}_r$
and the number of extant tips $n_r$. Point accuracy is summarised by:

\begin{align}
  \text{Bias}_p &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n} (\hat{\pars}_{r,p} - \pars^*_p),
  \label{eq:bias}\\
  \text{RMSE}_p &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{r=1}^{n}
    (\hat{\pars}_{r,p} - \pars^*_p)^2}.
  \label{eq:rmse}
\end{align}

The tree-size effect is visualised by plotting $|\hat{\pars}_{r,p} - \pars^*_p|$
against $n_r$ with a LOWESS smooth. Consistent improvement with $n_r$
confirms that the IS estimator is consistent: larger trees provide a sharper
likelihood surface and more informative augmentation.

\subsection{Model selection power}\label{sec:study2}

We assess the ability of AIC-based selection to recover the true
data-generating model in a fully crossed design. Twenty-five trees are
simulated from each of the four pure models (CR, DD, PD, EP), giving
$100$ trees in total. All four models are then fitted to every tree, and
the model with the lowest AIC is selected.

The results are summarised in a $4 \times 4$ confusion matrix
\begin{equation}
  C_{ij} = \#\bigl\{\text{trees generated by model }i
             \text{ for which model }j\text{ was selected}\bigr\},
\end{equation}
and the row-normalised version $C_{ij}/\sum_j C_{ij}$ (recall matrix).
Overall accuracy is $\mathrm{acc} = \operatorname{tr}(C)/\sum_{i,j}C_{ij}$.
Per-model recall $C_{ii}/\sum_j C_{ij}$ measures how reliably each true
model is identified.

\section{Mathematical Appendix}\label{sec:appendix}

\subsection{Derivation of the log-sum-exp identity}

For numerical stability, \eqref{eq:fhat} is never evaluated as
$\log(\frac{1}{S}\sum_i \exp(\lw_i))$ directly because the exponentials
can overflow or underflow. Instead, factor out $w_{\max} = \max_i \lw_i$:
\begin{align}
  \log\!\left(\frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^S e^{\lw_i}\right)
  &= \log\!\left(\frac{e^{w_{\max}}}{S}
      \sum_{i=1}^S e^{\lw_i - w_{\max}}\right) \nonumber\\
  &= w_{\max} + \log\!\left(\frac{1}{S}\sum_{i=1}^S e^{\lw_i - w_{\max}}\right).
\end{align}
Since $\lw_i - w_{\max} \leq 0$, all terms $\exp(\lw_i - w_{\max}) \in (0,1]$
are safe to evaluate.

\subsection{ESS in log-space}

From the normalised weights $\tilde{w}_i = \exp(\lw_i) / \sum_j \exp(\lw_j)$,
the ESS \eqref{eq:ESS} equals $1/\sum_i \tilde{w}_i^2$. In log-space:
\begin{equation}
  \ESS = \frac{\left(\sum_i e^{\lw_i}\right)^2}{\sum_i e^{2\lw_i}}
  = \exp\!\left[2\,\mathrm{lse}(\mathbf{w}) - \mathrm{lse}(2\mathbf{w})\right],
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{lse}(\mathbf{v}) = \max_i v_i + \log\sum_i e^{v_i - \max_j v_j}$.

\subsection{Annealing decay rate}

If $\bm{\sigma}^{(0)} = c\mathbf{1}$ for some $c > 0$ and
$\bm{\sigma}^{(k+1)} = \bm{\sigma}^{(k)} - \alpha\mathbf{1}$, the
population collapses (all $\sigma_p^{(k)} \leq 0$) at iteration
$k^* = \lceil c/\alpha \rceil$. Setting $k^* = T$ gives the default
annealing step $\alpha = c/T = \sigma_p^{(0)}/T$.
